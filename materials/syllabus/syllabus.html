Course Description

An introduction to the planning, organizing, designing and building of usable, accessible and beautiful web sites using industry-standard design methods and modern, semantic development techniques. Students will be exposed to a user-centered design process that will aid them in balancing aesthetics with user experience and will learn to build their designs for the web using HTML5, CSS3 and jQuery. Prerequisites: CDGD-220.

Course Objectives

This course introduces the planning, organizing, designing and building of websites at a basic level using industry-standard design methods and modern, semantic development techniques.

Students will learn to identify site needs and audiences, organize content, mockup site designs, code their websites in HTML and CSS, and publish it online.

Students should expect to develop the following skills:
  • Plan, design and develop a new or redesigned website using best practices.
  • Collaborate in teams during design and development of websites.
  • Organize site content suitable for continued development.
  • Understand modern web interface design, principles of usability and accessibility.
  • Understand the how the underlying technologies of the web work and how they influence the design process.
  • Ability to style and layout websites using CSS.
  • Able to seek out solutions online and continue their web education independently.

Methodologies

  • Lectures introduce the conceptual foundations of web design and usability as well as the technical underpinnings of web design.
  • Demonstrations, apprenticeship, workshopping and pair programming teach and re-enforce programming and design skills.
  • Pair programming leverages peer-learning as well as provides a platform for practicing collaboration skills.
  • Assignments allow for independent practice of skills.
  • Readings support theoretical understanding, build foundations and prepare students for in-class lectures.

Grading

Throughout this semester you will receive many assignments, each with various stages and parts. Students should treat all of their assignments as a single body of work in which they improve over the entire semester. Assignments deadlines are very important to keep you on track, but also, continued progress and iteration on all assignments is a vital aspect of this class that will help you grow as a web developer.

  1. 40% Assignment Initial Submission

    All work is due on time and pushed to GitHub (get assignments from classmates or course website if you miss class). Expect to work at least 5 hours outside of class time per week on readings and design assignments. If you need to, spend extra time in the 3rd floor Digital Media Labs. Always be prepared to present for class review all assignments. All assignments are due by the start of class.

  2. 40% Assignment Revisions

    Web design and programming are processes that benefit from review and revision. Students should be responsive and timely to suggestions and requests for revisions from the teacher. Requests and suggestions will be made in class, via email, via assignments and via GitHub issues and pull requests.

  3. 20% Class Participation & Attendance

    Students are expected to arrive on time, stay until the class is dismissed and fully participate in every class. This includes, but is not limited to, participating in class conversations, providing feedback during critiques and working respectfully during pair programming.

Class Structure (typical)

9—10:45 | UX, Technical Apprenticeship

  • Observation, critical analysis. Students independently perform a user experience (UX) audit of a given website
  • Feedback, questions, apprenticeship. Teacher reviews student's assignments and revisions individually while other students work on the audits and their revisions.
  • Presentation, communication, sharing, inspiration. Teacher chooses several people randomly to speak about their UX findings.
  • Homework review. Students volunteer to review their homework in front of the class.

10:45—11:15 | 30 minute break

11:15-12 | New Concepts

  • Conceptual and technical instruction. Teacher chooses several questions and comments from the previous week's readings to discuss.
  • Conceptual or technical lecture. Teacher presents a conceptual or technical lecture.
  • Examples. Teacher presents prepared examples or live-codes.

12—1 | Pair Programming

  • New homework assignment is presented.
  • Collaboration, peer-learning, coding practice. Students are assigned programming partners randomly. Pairs perform assigned exercises, resolve outstanding GitHub issues or work on assignment revisions.

Plagiarism

Plagiarism In creative work, plagiarism is the inappropriate and unethical representation of another's work as one's own. In those instances where a significant portion of a creative work is intentionally "appropriated," plagiarism is the failure to note, orally or in writing, the source of the appropriation. In expository or academic writing, whenever your work incorporates someone else's research, images, words, or ideas, you must properly identify the source unless you can reasonably expect knowledgeable people to recognize it. Proper citation gives credit where it is due and enables your readers to locate sources and pursue lines of inquiry raised by your paper. Students who do not comply may be penalized.
